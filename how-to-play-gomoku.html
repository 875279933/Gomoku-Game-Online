How to Play Gomoku Game

Learn how to play Gomoku in 5 minutes. The most basic rules, simple winning strategies, and common beginner mistakes. Perfect for beginners who have never played Gomoku before. Whether you're learning how to play Gomoku for the first time or looking to improve your skills, this guide will walk you through everything you need to know about how to play Gomoku effectively.

1. What do you need to play Gomoku?

To learn how to play Gomoku, you first need the right equipment:

  • A board: Standard Gomoku uses a 15×15 grid, but any grid-based board will work, such as a Go board or graph paper. Knowing how to play Gomoku starts with understanding the board layout.
  • Black and white pieces: One color per player—enough to cover most of the intersections.

Tip: You can even draw a grid on paper, use coins and small stones as pieces, or play for free online. Learning how to play Gomoku doesn't require expensive equipment!

2. The Basic Rules (3 Sentences)

Now that you have your setup, let's cover the core rules of how to play Gomoku:

  • First Move: Black goes first. Black places the first black stone on any intersection. This is fundamental to understanding how to play Gomoku.
  • Turns: Black and White take turns placing stones, with only one stone allowed per turn. Rhythm is key when learning how to play Gomoku.
  • Winning: The player who first connects five of their own stones in a row—horizontally, vertically, or diagonally—wins. That's the ultimate goal when learning how to play Gomoku!

That's it. There is no capturing or taking stones; players continue playing until one side connects five stones. This simplicity is what makes learning how to play Gomoku so accessible.

Q: Does connecting six or more stones count as a win?

In casual play (free rules), connecting six or more stones counts as a win. In standard tournament rules (Renju), there are restrictions for Black (long chains do not count as a win, and there are rules like the "three-in-a-row" prohibition), but beginners can safely ignore these and simply play by the "five-in-a-row wins" rule. When starting to learn how to play Gomoku, focus on the basic rules first.

3. Getting Started (Get Started in One Minute)

Ready to put your knowledge of how to play Gomoku into practice? Follow these steps:

  • Place the board between the two players.
  • Decide who plays Black; Black has a slight first-move advantage. This is an important point to remember when learning how to play Gomoku.
  • Black usually makes the first move near the center of the board (for example, at point H8 on a 15×15 board). Central positioning is key when learning how to play Gomoku.
  • White places a white stone anywhere on the board, and then the players take turns.

4. Simple and Easy-to-Understand Attack and Defense Strategies (To Help You Win More Games)

Attack – How to Take the Initiative to Win

Knowing how to play Gomoku is about more than just placing stones—you need strategy:

  • Control the Center: Stones in the center of the board can develop in more directions. This is a key principle when learning how to play Gomoku.
  • Create a "Double Threat": Try to make a move that simultaneously creates a "live three" and a "charging four" (or two live threes). Your opponent can only block one side, and you win. Mastering this tactic is crucial to how to play Gomoku effectively.
  • Jump a Space: Occasionally jumping a space (similar to a knight's move) can disrupt your opponent's defensive rhythm. This advanced technique will elevate how you play Gomoku.

Defense – How to Stop Your Opponent

Learning how to play Gomoku isn't just about attacking—it's about defending too:

  • Block One End Immediately Upon Seeing a Live Four: If your opponent has connected four stones with no blocks on either end, you must block one end on your next move (otherwise, your opponent will form a five-in-a-row on the next turn). This is a critical defensive move when learning how to play Gomoku.
  • Keep an eye on live triples: When your opponent forms a live triple, immediately block one end to prevent it from becoming a live quadruple. Anticipation is key to how to play Gomoku well.
  • Common traps: Don't just focus on the immediate threat; watch for a "double kill" lurking behind it. This awareness separates casual players from those who truly know how to play Gomoku.

Opening tips: For the first 3–5 moves, try to group your stones in the center area; don't scatter them all over the board. This foundational advice will improve how you play Gomoku from the very start.

5. The simplest three-step winning strategy

Now that you understand how to play Gomoku, here's a foolproof strategy to win:

  1. Create a live three (three stones in a straight line with both ends free). This is the first step in executing how to play Gomoku offensively.
  2. On the next move, turn this live three into a live four (four stones with both ends free). This progression is essential to mastering how to play Gomoku.
  3. Since your opponent cannot block both ends at the same time, play your fifth stone to form a line of five and win.

This is the classic two-move kill. Once you master this sequence, you'll truly understand how to play Gomoku strategically.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q: Is Gomoku the same as Go?
A: It's completely different. Go is about capturing territory and capturing stones, while Gomoku is simply about who can form a line of five first. Knowing this distinction is important when learning how to play Gomoku.
Q: Who goes first?
A: Black goes first. When playing casually, you can choose the first move by chance or play rock-paper-scissors. This is a basic rule to remember when learning how to play Gomoku.
Q: Can you play on a chessboard?
A: Yes, you can play on any board at least 8×8 in size. Use coins, bottle caps, or differently colored buttons as pieces. Flexibility is part of what makes how to play Gomoku so accessible.
Q: How long does a game typically last?
A: For beginners, 5–15 minutes; experienced players can finish a game on a 15×15 board in 2–5 minutes. The speed of play is something you'll get used to as you learn how to play Gomoku.
