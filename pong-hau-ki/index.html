What Is Pong Hau K'i?

Pong Hau K'i — sometimes called Qu-Zi Chess — is a traditional Chinese blocking game played on a five-point board. Each player controls two pieces and slides them along connecting lines. The first player who cannot move loses.

The game has been played in Chinese villages for generations. Farmers carved the five-point board into dirt during lunch breaks, using pebbles or seeds as pieces. The rules fit on a napkin, but a single careless slide can leave you with no legal move. That tension is what keeps the game interesting after hundreds of plays.

How to Play Pong Hau K'i

White moves first. Select one of your pieces, then slide it to the next open point along a line. Click-to-select and click-to-destination both work, or drag the piece directly. After your move, the AI takes its turn. The game ends when one side has no legal move available — that side loses.

The center point is the strongest position on the board. A piece in the middle can reach any corner in a single move, while a piece on an edge is limited to two options. Whoever holds the center controls the pace of the game.

Winning Strategies for Beginners

Control the Center

A piece in the middle can slide to any open corner in one move. An opponent confined to the edges is limited to one or two options, and the board opens up for whoever holds the middle.

Force Your Opponent Into a Corner

Push both AI pieces toward the same side of the board. Once they are squeezed into two adjacent corners with no path back to the middle, their choices become limited and predictable.

Think Two Moves Ahead

Before each slide, check where the computer can go on the next turn. Strong play comes from setting up positions three or four moves in advance and leaving the opponent with only losing options.

The History Behind Pong Hau K'i

Pong Hau K'i has been played in Chinese villages for generations. Farmers carved the five-point board into dirt during lunch breaks, using pebbles or seeds as pieces. The name describes the goal: blocking and trapping.

Some regions call it Qu-Zi Chess, after the fixed paths pieces must follow. The game has no commercial history and no championship circuit. It survived on its own because it is genuinely fun to play. This browser version keeps that tradition — load the page and the board is ready.
