What Is Pong Hau K'i?

Pong Hau K'i — sometimes called Qu-Zi Chess — is a blocking game from rural China that fits on a napkin. The board has just five points. Each player gets two pieces. You slide one piece at a time along the lines, and whoever traps the other side first wins.

I first came across this game in an old book on Chinese folk games. The rules looked almost too simple, but after a few rounds against a friend I realized how easy it is to paint yourself into a corner. That is the hook — you think you are safe, then suddenly you have nowhere to go. This page lets you play against the computer whenever you want, no setup required.

How to Play Pong Hau K'i

White moves first. Pick a piece and slide it to the next open point along a line. You can click to select, then click your destination, or just drag the piece. After you move, the computer takes its turn. The game ends when one side is boxed in with no legal move left — that side loses.

The center point is everything. From there you can reach any corner in one move, while your opponent is stuck on the edges. Grab it early, and fight to keep it.

Rules at a Glance

  • White (you) starts at the bottom, Black (AI) at the top
  • Move one piece per turn to an adjacent empty point along a line
  • No jumping over pieces — only direct moves along connected lines
  • The first player who cannot move loses the game
  • Play with mouse clicks or drag-and-drop on any device

Winning Strategies for Beginners

Control the Center

Sit a piece in the middle and you can jump to any open corner in a single move. Your opponent, hugging the edges, is suddenly down to one or two choices. It is the simplest way to tighten the noose.

Force Your Opponent Into a Corner

Push both AI pieces onto the same side of the board. Once they are squeezed into two adjacent corners with no path back to the middle, their options dry up fast.

Think Two Moves Ahead

Before you slide, ask where the computer can go next. The best players set traps three or four turns out. Every move feels like solving a tiny puzzle.

The History Behind Pong Hau K'i

Pong Hau K'i has been around Chinese villages for longer than anyone can remember. Farmers used to scratch the five-point board into dirt during lunch breaks, using pebbles or seeds as pieces. It was never about fancy equipment — just a quick way to pass time and keep the mind sharp.

The name itself hints at what you are doing: blocking and trapping. Some areas call it Qu-Zi Chess because the pieces stick to fixed paths, much like pieces in other old strategy games.
