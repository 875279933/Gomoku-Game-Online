What Is Pong Hau K'i?

Pong Hau K'i (also called "Qu-Zi Chess") is one of the simplest yet most clever board games to come out of rural China. The board has only 5 points. Each side controls 2 pieces. You take turns sliding one piece at a time along the connecting lines. The catch? You win by trapping your opponent so they have nowhere to move.

It takes about thirty seconds to learn the rules, but you will spend hours figuring out how to outthink the other player. We built this online version so you can practice against an AI opponent anytime, right in your browser.

How to Play Pong Hau K'i

White always moves first. Pick one of your pieces and slide it to the next open point along a line. You can either click a piece and then click where you want it to go, or just drag it straight there. After your move, the AI responds. The game keeps going until one side gets stuck with no legal moves — that player loses.

The center point is the most important spot on the board. Whoever controls it usually has the upper hand, so try to grab it early and don't give it up without a fight.

Rules at a Glance

  • White (you) starts at the bottom, Black (AI) at the top
  • Move one piece per turn to an adjacent empty point along a line
  • No jumping over pieces — only direct moves along connected lines
  • The first player who cannot move loses the game
  • Play with mouse clicks or drag-and-drop on any device

Winning Strategies for Beginners

Control the Center

The middle point connects to all four corners. If your piece sits there, you can reach any empty spot on the board in one move. Your opponent, stuck on the edges, will have far fewer options.

Force Your Opponent Into a Corner

Try to push both of the AI's pieces onto the same side of the board. Once they are squeezed into two adjacent corners with no way to the center, it is only a matter of time before they run out of moves.

Think Two Moves Ahead

Before you slide a piece, ask yourself: "If I move here, where can the AI go next?" The best players plan their traps several turns in advance. It feels a lot like solving a small puzzle every time it is your turn.

The History Behind Pong Hau K'i

This game has been played in Chinese villages for generations, long before anyone had a smartphone. Farmers would scratch the board into dirt or draw it on paper during breaks. It was their way of passing time and sharpening their minds.

The name "Pong Hau K'i" roughly translates to a game of blocking and trapping. Some regions call it "Qu-Zi Chess" because the pieces move along fixed paths, similar to how pieces follow routes in other traditional strategy games.
