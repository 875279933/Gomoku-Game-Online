What Is Gomoku?

Gomoku, also called Five in a Row, is a classic two-player board game played on a 15x15 grid. Players alternate placing black and white stones at empty intersections. The first to line up five stones in an unbroken row — horizontally, vertically, or diagonally — wins.

The rules fit on a napkin, but real competition takes years of practice. This version pairs you with a capable AI opponent, ready to play directly in your browser. No download, no account, no setup.

How to Play

Black moves first. Click any open intersection to place a stone, and the AI replies on its turn. Connect five stones in a row to win.

Use Undo to take back your most recent move along with the AI's reply. Use New Game to reset the board. Difficulty can be changed at any time — Easy plays a relaxed game, Hard punishes mistakes and rewards solid opening play.

Key Strategies

  • Four-Three Fork: Build a position that threatens two winning lines at once. The opponent can only block one.
  • Live Three: Three stones in a row with both ends open. This must be blocked on the next move or it becomes unstoppable.
  • Control the Center: Stones near the middle have more empty space around them, which means more ways to extend a line.
  • Read Ahead: Strong play depends on looking two or three moves forward and forcing the opponent into a losing response.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does the AI work?+
Easy picks mostly random moves with basic defense. Medium balances attack and defense. Hard runs a 5-ply search and actively looks for double threats. The gap between levels is clear after a few games.
Can I undo multiple moves?+
Yes. Each click of Undo removes your most recent move together with the AI's reply. Keep clicking to rewind further.
Does changing difficulty reset my score?+
No, win and loss counts are kept across difficulty levels. Use the Reset button next to the score to clear them.
Why does Hard AI take longer?+
It runs more calculation per move — threat detection, position evaluation, and search for forced wins. A small delay is added so the response feels natural rather than instant.
What happens if the board fills up?+
The game ends in a draw with no points awarded. Draws are uncommon and only occur when both sides defend every threat.
